The Failure Equation

I have a mathematical theory that everyone will fail at some point of their life or another. This is not to say you should actively seek out to failure, that's useless. But, while it's net non-beneficial to learn to fail, this toes a dangerous line with failing to learn.

But, I think it's critical to get used to developing an appetite for the risk of failure early on in life. I've always thought of failure in similarity to the torque equation:

where n = your current age > 13 and F = the magnitude of impact of the event that caused the failure in life. This can be explained below between the lever and impact parts of the equation.

The Lever of the Equation (n - 13) ^ (n - 13)

The first part of the equation features n - 13 as the base under the presumption that the lever of failure in this case is your (current age - 13), or rather, your current age - the earliest age after which your actions start having real consequences. This happens to coincide with the average age a student starts high school in America. Basically this presumes that you can almost entirely mess up your life before you start high school and the results will be inconsequential.

But after you start high school, the effect of your failure isn't just driven by the number of years since consequences of actions started mattering, but are actually magnified by the amount of stability you've derived. This is the exponential part of the equation. Every year past the page of 13, you start to gain more stability in a life of fluidity. By 15 you figure out what you start to figure out what like, who you like, what your interests are. By the time you're 20 and in college you decide a major, you pick your field of discipline to study, you start to form a group of your life long friends. By your mid 20's you settle into a career, by your 30's you start to get married and have kids, and the clock doesn't stop ticking every year after that.

The Impact of the Equation (x F)

This is all multiplied by the magnitude of the impact of the failure, F, be it failing a test, failing to get a job you so badly wanted, or failing to achieve anything you've wanted but couldn't get. This failure strikes impact on the lever, and contributes to the impact of the failure.

But, what's important to realize is that the actual failure itself is so inconsequential in comparison to when failure happens.

Under the assumption that there's an inherent correlation between risk and failure (anything you seek to achieve that requires risk also possesses an innate chance of failing), this explains why its so much harder to take risks as you get older.

For every year you progress in life, it becomes a bit harder to make the jump off the cliff for fear of failure. You're not only older, but your life becomes more stable, and those two compound to grow at an exponential rate of stagnancy. We all have success we hope to some day achieve. When you're a kid, these things are called dreams: dreams of one day writing a book, running for president, becoming a professional athlete, starting a venture, opening a coffeeshop. Yet, every year we fail to act on those dreams, we continue to make it harder to take a leap of faith to start those dreams because our perceived failure impact increases.

Fight the failure equation; fail early and realize that almost any failure is bounce-able back from. That failure helps shape who you are, often teaches you more lessons than success, and that the fear of failing, not failure itself, is that which stands between you and achieving what you hope to in life.
